# Real-world outcomes of consolidative durvalumab after concurrent chemoradiotherapy in stage III non-small cell lung cancer: the prognostic role of clinical frailty in the post-PACIFIC era.

BACKGROUND: Consolidative durvalumab following concurrent chemoradiotherapy 
(CCRT) is the current standard of care for unresectable stage III non-small cell 
lung cancer (NSCLC). However, survival outcomes in real-world populations remain 
variable. We evaluated the independent association between consolidative 
durvalumab and survival outcomes in a real-world cohort.
METHODS: This retrospective single-center study included 108 patients with 
unresectable stage III NSCLC treated with definitive CCRT between 2017 and 2022. 
Overall survival (OS) and progression-free survival (PFS) were analyzed using 
Kaplan-Meier estimates and multivariable Cox regression models. Baseline 
Clinical Frailty Scale (CFS) scores were assigned retrospectively. Multivariable 
logistic regression was performed to assess factors associated with durvalumab 
initiation.
RESULTS: Among 108 patients, 50 received consolidative durvalumab and 58 did 
not. Median follow-up was 16.5 months. In multivariable analysis, durvalumab was 
independently associated with improved OS [hazard ratio (HR) 0.16, 95% 
confidence interval (CI): 0.06-0.39; P<0.001]. No independent association was 
observed between durvalumab and PFS (HR 0.84, 95% CI: 0.44-1.58; P=0.59). In 
patients with programmed death-ligand 1 (PD-L1) expression ≥1%, durvalumab 
remained independently associated with improved OS (HR 0.43, 95% CI: 0.21-0.87; 
P=0.02), but not PFS. Baseline frailty was not independently associated with 
durvalumab initiation. Grade ≥3 adverse events occurred in 8.3% of patients, 
with no grade 4-5 toxicities observed.
CONCLUSIONS: In this real-world cohort, consolidative durvalumab was 
independently associated with improved OS, including in patients with PD-L1 
expression ≥1%. The absence of an independent PFS association underscores the 
complexity of survival interpretation in heterogeneous clinical populations.
